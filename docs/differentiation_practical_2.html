9  Gene regulation in space

How wonderful that we have met with a paradox.
Now we have some hope of making progress – Niehls Bohr

9.1 Introduction

In this practical you will study the effects of auxin, a plant hormone, on gene regulation in the root. Auxin is a hormone that is virtually involved in all developmental processes in plants, from embryo patterning and floral organ determination to leaf disposition and root growth. In each context, auxin triggers specific responses, which can be explained by the underlying regulatory networks. Indeed, there are multiple molecular players in the auxin signalling pathway that can be expressed in different tissues. Particularly important are the the auxin response factors, ARFs, that are transcription factors that regulate the expression of genes in response to auxin.

In the root, researchers have found an auxin paradox: some experiments show that auxin promotes the expression of the gene WOX5 (a root stem cell transcription factor), while others found it represses WOX5 (Figure 1). This opposite auxin regulation is mediated by different ARFs—ARF5 and ARF10—posing the possibility that accounting for how ARF5 and ARF10 are themselves regulated can reconcile the results. Here we will explore this possibility using an in silico root model; we will address this paradox and propose an explanation.

9.2 Biological background

Plant roots grow thanks to the presence of stem cells housed in a niche at the root tip. A subset of these cells express WOX5, maintaining the surrounding cells undifferentiated. WOX5 is regulated by several factors, and network models help us understand how.

The Boolean network we used last week describes the gene and hormonal activity configurations of the cells in the root tip In this model WOX5 is regulated positively by ARF5, and negatively by ARF10 and CLE40. Both ARF5 and ARF10 are auxin response factors, activated by auxin. Moreover, other root transcription factors control ARF5 and ARF10 expression causing them to be expressed in specific cells of the root. This regulation is included in the Boolean model we studied last week. Here you will explore whether this is relevant to understanding the auxin–WOX5 paradox. For this you will use an in silico root model in which each cell carries a copy of the Boolean network initialized in a particular attractor, which allows us to test hypotheses and find an explanation.

9.3 The model

The model simulates the cells of a root tip, with a stem cell niche at the very tip. Cells are organized in cell layers, and are coupled with each other via the transport of auxin. Each cell in the model has a regulatory network, that defines their cell types and the direction at which they transport auxin, and the expression pattern of the regulators: ARF10, ARF5, MGP and WOX5. (lime green cells in the figure below; purple cells in the WOX5 visualization). The different tissues of the root are spatially organized. Each cell has a different set of expressed genes, which we account for by initializing each cell’s Boolean network in the QC, Vascular, Endodermis, and Columella attractors. (Cortex and epidermis are not modeled.) Multiple grids store different cell properties: cell-type, auxin levels, and expression of each gene.

You can visualize ARF10, ARF5, MGP, and WOX5 expression using the provided plotting function.
